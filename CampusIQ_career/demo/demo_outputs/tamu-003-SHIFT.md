## 2. WHAT IS SHIFTING

### Being Automated — Tasks That Are Compressing

These are not disappearing overnight, but they represent a meaningful shift in what employers expect entry-level hires to bring beyond them.

**1. First-draft content generation**
Writing social captions, basic ad copy, email templates, and boilerplate press release language are now tasks AI tools can produce in seconds. Employers are increasingly less likely to hire a coordinator to spend four hours drafting copy — they expect that output to be faster and for the human on the team to be shaping and evaluating it, not just producing it.

**2. Templated graphic and asset production**
Producing routine visual assets using preset brand templates — social graphics, promotional banners, standard slide decks — is being handled faster and at higher volume through AI-assisted design tools. Entry-level roles that were primarily execution-focused in this lane are thinning.

**3. Routine performance reporting**
Compiling basic engagement metrics (likes, reach, click-through rates) and packaging them into standard reports is increasingly automated through platform-native dashboards and AI summaries. The task of pulling the numbers is compressing; the task of interpreting them is not.

---

### Growing or Transforming — Where Human Contribution Is Holding and Expanding

**1. Brand voice, audience judgment, and editorial decision-making**
AI can generate content, but it cannot consistently make the judgment call about what a brand should and should not say to a specific audience at a specific moment. That requires understanding brand identity, cultural context, and audience trust — all of which are fundamentally human communication skills. This is where your storytelling and creative thinking background becomes genuinely valuable, not decorative.

**2. Strategic content planning and campaign architecture**
Deciding *why* a campaign exists, what it is trying to accomplish, which audiences it should reach and through what sequence of touchpoints — this is the work that's growing. Employers increasingly want coordinators and associates who can think strategically, not just execute tactically. Your PR Principles and Marketing coursework gives you a foundation here that you can build on intentionally.

**3. Relationship-driven communication: media, stakeholders, community**
In PR and brand communications specifically, relationships with journalists, community stakeholders, and brand partners require human trust-building that AI cannot replicate. The earned media side of communications — pitching, relationship management, reputation navigation — is a durable and distinctly human lane.

> **Where AI fluency amplifies:** In all three of these areas, knowing how to use AI tools to work faster — drafting faster, researching faster, prototyping faster — means you spend more of your time on the judgment work and less on the production work. That is the actual competitive advantage: not that you use AI, but that using AI frees you to do the higher-skill work better.

## 3. WHAT TO LEARN

Sequenced by what matters most, not what sounds most current.

---

### PRIORITY 1: Strategic Communication Thinking
**Why it matters for this field:**
The market context is clear that employers rank critical thinking and communication first. For marketing and PR specifically, this means being able to connect a communication decision to a business or brand objective — not just "we should post this" but "here is why this message, for this audience, through this channel, moves us toward this goal." This is the skill gap that separates coordinators who get promoted from those who plateau.

**How to develop it concretely:**
- In your PR Principles and Marketing coursework, practice framing every assignment not just as "what should we communicate" but "why does this matter to this specific audience and what should they do or think differently." Write that reasoning out explicitly.
- Follow 2-3 DFW-based brands on LinkedIn and audit their campaigns — what audiences are they targeting, what message are they sending, and why do you think they made those choices? Build the habit of thinking strategically about work you can observe.
- Seek out case competition teams at Texas A&M (the Marketing or Communication departments often have them). These simulate exactly this kind of decision-making under realistic constraints.

---

### PRIORITY 2: Audience-Grounded Content Strategy
**Why it matters for this field:**
This bridges your existing content creation skills with the more durable, strategic version of the work. There is a meaningful difference between making content and developing a content strategy — the latter involves audience research, platform judgment, message sequencing, and measurement. That distinction is what separates the roles that are compressing from the ones that are not.

**How to develop it concretely:**
- Take on a real project — a student organization, a small local business, a campus brand — and manage their content presence with a documented strategy: defined audience, defined goals, platform rationale, and a plan to measure what's working. Do this before you apply for internships. It becomes your portfolio.
- When you take upper-division courses in digital marketing or communication strategy, treat every project as an opportunity to build toward this. Don't just complete the assignment — document your reasoning so you can speak to it.

---

### PRIORITY 3: Structured AI Fluency for Communication Workflows
**Why it matters for this field:**
You already use AI tools for content ideas and captions. That gives you a real head start — but there is a gap between using AI and being able to explain *how* you use it, *why* you use it for specific tasks, and what you are bringing to the output that the tool cannot. That explainability is what the market context identifies as the actual gap: 85% of recent graduates use AI tools, but only 28% received any structured instruction. Employers can tell the difference.

**How to develop it concretely:**
- Start documenting your AI-assisted workflow deliberately. When you use a tool to generate content ideas or draft captions, write down what you prompted, what the output was, what you changed, and why. This builds the habit of critical evaluation — and it gives you specific examples to reference in interviews.
- Look for one structured course, workshop, or certification that addresses AI in a marketing or communications context specifically. Your university's career center (when you engage with it) or LinkedIn Learning are reasonable starting points. The goal is not to become technical — it is to build a vocabulary and framework for what you are already doing.
- Note: tools beyond what you currently use (Canva AI, social scheduling tools with AI features) exist, but approach new tools only after you have a clear use case for them. Adding tools without a strategy is not the same as building fluency.

---

**Interview talking point (when asked about AI or working with new tools):**

What I've learned is that the tool gets you to a starting point faster, but the judgment call about what actually fits the brand or the audience still has to come from you. I've been intentional about developing that editorial eye alongside the tool use."*

This is grounded in what you actually do. It demonstrates self-awareness about the human contribution — which is exactly what employers are trying to evaluate. Do not overclaim technical depth you do not have; this framing does not require it.
